Get Involved

Community Justice League is powered by neighbors helping neighbors. Our volunteer-driven approach means that every person who joins us brings their unique gifts, experiences, and perspectives to the vital work of community healing. Whether you have five hours a month or can commit to ongoing mentorship, there's a meaningful way for you to contribute.

Why Volunteer with Us?

When you volunteer with Community Justice League, you become part of a movement that believes in the power of connection, the possibility of transformation, and the strength of community. You'll join a network of dedicated individuals who understand that true safety comes not from isolation and punishment, but from building relationships and addressing the root causes of harm.

Our volunteers consistently tell us that this work has changed their lives as much as the lives of those they serve. You'll gain new perspectives, develop valuable skills, and experience the profound satisfaction that comes from being part of genuine healing and positive change in your community.

Volunteer Opportunities

Community Mentors

Making a Lasting Difference Through Relationship

As a community mentor, you'll be paired with an individual at various stages of the criminal justice system, providing the kind of consistent, caring support that can truly change a life. This is our most intensive volunteer commitment and our most transformative opportunity.

What Community Mentors Do:

  • Build authentic, long-term relationships with mentees based on trust and mutual respect
  • Provide practical support with housing, employment, education, and accessing community services
  • Offer guidance and encouragement as mentees work toward their personal goals
  • Help mentees develop life skills and navigate community systems
  • Connect mentees to resources, opportunities, and social networks
  • Accompany mentees to appointments, court dates, or community events when appropriate
  • Model healthy relationships and positive community engagement

The Mentor Commitment:

We ask mentors to commit to at least one year of regular contact with their mentee, typically involving 4-6 hours per month of direct contact plus preparation and follow-up time. This might include weekly phone calls, bi-weekly meetings, and participation in group activities or community events.

Who Makes a Great Mentor:

  • Adults from all walks of life who believe in the potential for positive change
  • Individuals with patience, empathy, and strong listening skills
  • People who can maintain appropriate boundaries while building genuine relationships
  • Those willing to learn about trauma, addiction, and the impacts of incarceration
  • Community members who can offer practical knowledge about local resources and opportunities

Mentor Training and Support:

All mentors complete our comprehensive 20-hour training program covering restorative justice principles, trauma-informed care, effective communication, boundary setting, and community resources. We provide ongoing monthly supervision, peer support groups, and continuing education opportunities. You'll never be alone in this work.

Circle Facilitators

Guiding Communities Through Healing Dialogue

Circle facilitators are the skilled guides who create and hold safe space for our mediation circles and community healing processes. This role requires specific training but offers the opportunity to witness and support some of the most profound moments of accountability and reconciliation.

What Circle Facilitators Do:

  • Prepare all participants for circle processes through individual pre-circle meetings
  • Facilitate mediation circles between victims, offenders, and community members
  • Guide group discussions using restorative justice principles and circle methodology
  • Ensure all voices are heard and respected throughout the process
  • Help participants develop meaningful agreements for repair and accountability
  • Support follow-up processes and agreement monitoring when needed
  • Co-facilitate with other trained volunteers to ensure best practices

The Facilitator Commitment:

We ask mentors to commit to at least one year of regular contact with their mentee, typically involving 4-6 hours per month of direct contact plus preparation and follow-up time. This might include weekly phone calls, bi-weekly meetings, and participation in group activities or community events.

Who Makes a Great Facilitator:

  • Individuals with strong interpersonal and communication skills
  • People who can remain calm and centered in emotionally charged situations
  • Those with experience in conflict resolution, counseling, social work, or related fields (though not required)
  • Community members who can maintain neutrality while showing compassion for all participants
  • Individuals committed to ongoing learning and skill development

Facilitator Training and Certification:

Our facilitator training is a 40-hour program spread over several weekends, covering circle processes, trauma-informed facilitation, cultural competency, safety protocols, and extensive hands-on practice. After initial training, new facilitators are paired with experienced mentors for their first several cases. We provide ongoing advanced training and peer consultation opportunities.

Victim Advocates

Supporting Those Who Have Experienced Harm

Victim advocates provide specialized support to individuals who have experienced harm, ensuring their voices are heard and their needs are centered throughout any restorative process.

What Victim Advocates Do:

  • Provide individual support and information to victims about their options
  • Help victims understand restorative processes and their rights within them
  • Accompany victims to meetings, circles, or court proceedings when requested
  • Connect victims to community resources and support services
  • Ensure victim safety and wellbeing remain the priority in all processes

Training Requirements:

25-hour specialized training in victim services, trauma-informed care, and restorative practices.

Legal Services Volunteers

Helping Navigate Complex Systems

Legal services volunteers help their neighbours understand and navigate the criminal justice system, ensuring everyone has access to information, representation, and legal advice.

What Legal Services Volunteers Do:

  • Provide information about legal rights and processes
  • Assist with completing legal documents and paperwork
  • Accompany their neighbours to court hearings and legal appointments
  • Represent their neighbours in court and offer them legal advice
  • Advocate for restorative alternatives within the formal system
  • Connect their neighbours with qualified legal representation when needed

Training Requirements:

20-hour training covering basic legal processes, rights, and resources, plus ongoing consultation with legal professionals.

Community Outreach Volunteers

Spreading Awareness and Building Connections

Help us share the message of restorative justice and connect with community members who could benefit from or contribute to our work.

What Outreach Volunteers Do:

  • Staff information booths at community events and fairs
  • Deliver presentations to community organizations, schools, and faith communities
  • Develop and distribute educational materials
  • Build relationships with community partners and stakeholders
  • Support fundraising and awareness events

Training Requirements:

8-hour orientation to restorative justice principles and Community Justice League programs.
